How this ties into security & red teaming

We treat models like software and services: they accept inputs, produce outputs, and run in production. Our goal is to apply an offensive mindset to ML systems—ask how they can be fooled, how embeddings leak data, or how conjured inputs change outputs in hazardous ways. Practical areas we are exploring:

  • Adversarial examples and input-space manipulation against classifiers and LLMs
  • Data poisoning and training-set integrity (how bad data can backdoor a model)
  • Model hardening: detection, input sanitization, and thresholding strategies
  • Operational security for ML: secrets handling, access controls for model endpoints, and safe deployment patterns

Why word2vec still matters (short explainer)

Word2vec is a simple, powerful idea: represent words as vectors (numbers) so that similar words live near each other in that vector space. It taught us that meaning can be captured as geometry — arithmetic with vectors produces useful relationships (e.g., king − man + woman ≈ queen). That embedding idea is the conceptual ancestor of modern language-model tooling: embeddings are how models reason about similarity, how search & retrieval systems pick relevant context, and how many prompt-engineering tricks actually work. The CS50 language lecture’s final section on word2vec was a small but mind-blowing bridge from classic NLP to today’s transformer stacks.
